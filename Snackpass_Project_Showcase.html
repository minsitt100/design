Snackpass

How might we refine the rewards program and integrate gamification elements to make earning rewards more accessible and engaging for users?

Quick Summary

Problem:

Snackpass's current reward system is time-consuming and its gamification features are not incentivizing to keep users engaged for long periods.

Solutions:

I designed the "Favorite Store" feature, enabling users to request points from specific stores for easier gifting and reward redemption. I also enhanced Snackpass's "Chicken Coop" feature by allowing the in-app currency to be used for rewards. Additionally, I introduced "Leaderboards" to rank users by ordering and gifting activity, to create competitiveness and thereby user engagement.

Outcome:

The Co-Founder of Snackpass actually reviewed the project and reach out to me about it! He said he would keep it in mind for future consideration and enjoyed reading my insights. Check out the full deck I shared: Snackpass Deck.

So.. What is Snackpass?

Snackpass is a social food-pick-up app aimed at university students.

Users can order ahead for pickup, earn loyalty points for discounts, and gift friends directly through the app. It features exclusive discounts, an activity feed to track friends’ orders and gifts, and various gamification elements to enhance the experience and set it apart from the competition.

Snackpass users have been frustrated with the rewards program.

"Too many orders required to receive free rewards to incentivize me to take advantage of it.” - Surveyed Snackpass User.

After three years of using Snackpass, I've only managed to claim free rewards once due to the unattainable and slow nature of the current reward system. Consequently, I've found myself using Snackpass less frequently since there's little motivation unless it's purely convenient.

Additionally, some features felt out of place...

"The Chicken Coop feature feels random and initially confusing in how it relates to the rest of the app.” - Surveyed Snackpass User.

Chicken Coop is one of Snackpass’s gamification features that sets it apart from your typical food-pick up app.

The feature is straightforward: by ordering and sending points to friends, users fill a progress bar that eventually hatches a virtual chicken pet. Once hatched, the chicken generates coins that users can use to buy clothing and accessories for the pet.

While the feature is cute, that is all it really is. I felt the "Chicken Coop" gamification feature was disconnected from the app's core functions of pick-up and lacks appeal for users like myself who are uninterested in dressing up virtual chickens.

Thus, I asked:

How would I increase accessibility and statisfaction of the rewards system and modify and implement gamification elements to help Snackpass continue to set itself apart?

With a problem to solve, I sought user feedback to guide me towards solutions.

Before developing any high-fidelity prototypes, I surveyed 50+ Snackpass users to validate pain-points and solutions I had in mind. The survey aimed at understanding user gifting behavior, perspectives on the attainability of Snackpass's reward system, and engagement with the "Chicken Coop" feature.

Below are some main statistics and comments regarding user gifting behavior and the reward system:

  • 58.1% of users said they would want points from friends for a few specific stores they frequent.
  • 66.7% of users said Snackpass only feels slightly social.
  • 73.8% of users said they rarely use the Chicken Coop feature and see no benefit to it.

From these statistics, I had the following takeaways to guide my solutions:

  • Users prefer earning points from specific stores and like making progress towards them.
  • They want a more attainable rewards system.
  • Opportunities exist to use gamification features to improve engagement.
  • Update the Chicken Coop feature to be relevant to core functionality of the app: food-delivery and rewards.

The user surveys provided me the foundation to begin wireframing solutions.

With the takeaways in mind, I went on to wire-frame solutions that aimed to solve the pain-points I discovered. This lead me to three components of the project: Favorite Store, Leaderboards, Redesigning Chicken Coop.

Favorite Store Wireframes

Favorite Store Wireframe #1

Favorite Store Wireframe #2

Strategic Reasoning:

This feature allow users to set up a "Favorite Store" from their profile settings that they would like to get points from (Wireframe #1). This would then be communicated to all the user's friends during their chance to send a gift (Wireframe #2).

The goal is to generate more efficient point gifting and allow users to get points for stores they care about. Users would be prompted to send points to their friends who specifically request it, allowing their friends to get free rewards more often that they visit, rather than random stores they don't enjoy.

Desgin Choices (Set-up Flow):

Intuitive Placement: The set-up was intentionally placed in a user's profile settings, since this would feel the most intuitive for any new users wanting to adjust anything that uniquely pertains to them.

Confirmation Messaging: This helps reduce uncertainty about the store the user is about mark as their "favorite". It prevents them from making mistakes and having to go through process again.

User Flow for Favorite Store Set-Up

Design Choices (Gifting Flow):

Indicator: Friends that are requesting points from the store a user is about to gift is showcased at the top with a bright brand-consitent indicator. I did this so users are clearly presented the opportunity to make a decision with a positive impact.

Highlight Selection: By highlighting the specific friend the user selects, the design helps the user ensure they are sending their gift to the right person.

Leaderboard Wireframes

Leaderboard Wireframe #1

Leaderboard Wireframe #2

Strategic Reasoning:

Leaderboards traditionally is an effective gamification feature that makes users competiive by comparing themselevs with others as results are displayed publicly. Users at top rankings are able to feel a sense of accomplishment and recognition from other users, and thus are motivated to keep their ranks. In addition, casual users enjoy looking at leaderboards out of curiosity.

I saw an opportunity to implement this within Snackpass. Imagine seeing your friend on the top leaderboard for ordering 50 bobas within the span of 2 months! You reach out to them--both amazed and also perhaps concerned for their sugar intake. You could also take them up on the challenge and see if you can beat them on the rankings.

This is the goal for the feature: increase engagement and the social aspect of Snackpass by creating conversations between users and providing them more incentives to cotninue using the app.

In my design, I've provided the option for rankings under different categories, opening up multiple opportunities for users to rank, compete, and view information.

Design Choices:

Faimiliarity: I researched how leaderboards were structured in gaming apps which commonly rank player performance amongst sets of metrics. Through this, I adjusted the design (Wireframe #1 to Wireframe #2), making it concise and familiar.

Highlighting Top Players: I wanted top users to be clearly distinct and recognized, and thus applied a different color gradient from other users.

Intuitive Placement: I put "Leaderboards" within the same area as "Trending", a page where users can see what people are currently ordering from restaurants. "Trending" is currently the only page under the "lightning" icon in the app. Thus, putting "Leaderboards" under this icon would be intuitive, since both are data-oriented and informational pages.

Leaderboards & Trending Page

Chicken Coop Readjustment

Along with the data from the user surveys, I decided to make a mind-map to highlight key pain-points of the Chicken Coop Feature and ideate solutions. Primary pain-points from surveys was the feature lacked relevancy for the rest of the app and there is little incetive for users to interact with the feature.

Thus, the following solutions were ideated:

1. Link the "Favorite Store" feature to "Chicken Coop" by providing extra progress to hatching chicks if a user gives the points to their friends favorite store.

  • WHY: The feature would be connected to a core function of the app: ordering and delivering.

    • 2. A new gift-card system called SnackCards that allows users to convert coins collected from chickens into free rewards and discounts.

  • WHY: As the currency can only be gained by interacting with the feature, users are incetivized to use it if they want another method to obtain rewards.

    • 3. Leaderboards will have a category for the chicken coop feature such as most chickens hatched.

  • WHY: To allow users to be recognized for interacting and engaging with the app (since chickens are hatched through orders and gifting through Snackpass) and act as incetives for users that are competitive.

    • Design Choices:

    SncakCards: When designing the SnackCards, I used bright-colors and gradients to maintain the "fun" and "social" brand image that Snackpass has established itself with.

    Centralizing Features: In adjusting the Chicken Coop feature, I redesigned the page to have all related functions for the chickens—from collecting coins to shopping for new clothes—all on one page. Users now have access to everything they need front and center rather than navigating through different pages.

    Reflection

    This was my first experience in making a personal project and taking control of understanding and designing a product to imrpove it! I had a great learning experience in teaching myself Figma and understanding all the nunances behind the software. I also reseach mobile app design techniques and accesibility concepts to make sure that the designs I were making were as feasible as they could be.

    An aspect of the project I would have done differently if I had time, is interview more business owners! Users like me are one-side of the coin, so I would want to survey restaurant partners with Snackpass how the rewards system affects their business to see if an more effective middle-ground can be achieved.

    Regardless, I am super proud of what I was able to achieve and my ability to learn new techniques and push myself out of my comfort zone. Since this was a personal project, I designed all assets and aspects of the project on my own alongst managing my coursework. However, I kept pushing since I know the end-result of redesigning and thinking strategically on how to improve a product I use often is something I can be proud of!
